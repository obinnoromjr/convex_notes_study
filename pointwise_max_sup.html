Pointwise maximum and supremum

The first workhorse closure rule. A max — or a sup over any index set — of convex functions is convex; an inf of concave is concave. Its dual life is a proof method: write f as a sup of affine functions and convexity falls out for free.

fᵢ / f(·,y) — convex pieces g = max / supᵧ — the upper envelope epi g = ⋂ epi fᵢ — intersection p. 80–83 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

The third convexity-preserving operation, right after nonnegative weighted sums (§3.2.1) and affine precomposition (§3.2.2) — and the first genuinely rich one. The headline is short: the pointwise maximum of convex functions is convex, and this extends to the pointwise supremum over an arbitrary index set; dually, an infimum of concave functions is concave. What makes the rule a workhorse is its converse life as a method: to prove a function convex, exhibit it as a sup (or max) of affine or linear functions.

§1pointwise maximum — finite
If \(f_1,f_2\) are convex, then their pointwise maximum \[ f(x)=\max\{f_1(x),f_2(x)\},\qquad \operatorname{dom}f=\operatorname{dom}f_1\cap\operatorname{dom}f_2, \] is convex. This extends to any finite family \(f=\max\{f_1,\dots,f_m\}\).B&V §3.2.3 · p. 80
max of convex is convexconvexity + subadditivity of max
\[ f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)=\max_i f_i(\theta x+(1-\theta)y) \]
start from (3.1) applied to the max, on \(\operatorname{dom}f_1\cap\operatorname{dom}f_2\)
\[ \le\ \max_i\big(\theta f_i(x)+(1-\theta)f_i(y)\big) \]
each \(f_i\) convex. Bound every entry of the max before taking it — the max is monotone.
\[ \le\ \theta\max_i f_i(x)+(1-\theta)\max_i f_i(y) \]
subadditivity of max, and \(\theta,1-\theta\ge 0\): \(\max(a+b)\le\max a+\max b\)
\[ =\ \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y). \]
unfold the two maxima back into \(f\). ∎
+

Piecewise-linear functions are the archetype (Example 3.5): \(f(x)=\max\{a_1^{\mathsf T}x+b_1,\dots,a_L^{\mathsf T}x+b_L\}\) is convex, being a max of \(L\) affine functions. The kinks are exactly where the active piece switches.

§2pointwise supremum — infinite

The same closure holds for an arbitrary — possibly infinite — index set. This is the version that actually does the heavy lifting.

If \(f(x,y)\) is convex in \(x\) for each \(y\in\mathcal A\), then \[ g(x)=\sup_{y\in\mathcal A}f(x,y)\tag{3.7} \] is convex, with \(\operatorname{dom}g=\{x\mid (x,y)\in\operatorname{dom}f\ \forall y\in\mathcal A,\ \sup_{y}f(x,y)<\infty\}\).B&V §3.2.3 · p. 81
!

Direction is everything. sup of convex → convex; inf of concave → concave. The pointwise infimum of concave functions is concave — the exact dual statement. A sup of convex functions is convex but not, in general, concave, and an inf of convex functions is generally neither. Getting the curvature/operation pairing backwards is the classic trap.

  • sup ↔ convex
    Only \(x\)-convexity of each slice \(f(\cdot,y)\) is needed. No structure on the index set \(\mathcal A\) at all — it can be uncountable.
  • inf ↔ concave
    The mirror rule. Example 3.9 below is the canonical inf/concave case — watch that it is concave, not convex.
§3the epigraph view — the structural "why"

The cleanest reason the rule is true is geometric: a pointwise supremum corresponds to an intersection of epigraphs.

\[ \operatorname{epi}g=\bigcap_{y\in\mathcal A}\operatorname{epi}f(\cdot,y). \] Because \((x,t)\in\operatorname{epi}g\iff f(x,y)\le t\ \forall y\iff (x,t)\in\operatorname{epi}f(\cdot,y)\ \forall y\). Each \(\operatorname{epi}f(\cdot,y)\) is convex (§3.1.7), and an intersection of convex sets is convex (§2.3.1).B&V §3.2.3 · p. 81
!

Contrast with min. \(\operatorname{epi}(\min\{f_1,f_2\})=\operatorname{epi}f_1\cup\operatorname{epi}f_2\) — a union, and a union of convex sets is generally not convex. That is exactly why a min of convex functions need not be convex. Intersection preserves convexity; union does not.

§4the method, and the examples

B&V flag this as a "good method for establishing convexity": express \(f\) as a pointwise sup (or max) of affine — or linear — functions. The recurring trick is that \(y^{\mathsf T}Xy\) and \(u^{\mathsf T}Xv\) are linear in the matrix \(X\), which turns eigenvalue and norm quantities into sups of linear functions.

Example 3.5 · Piecewise-linearmax of affine

\(f(x)=\max\{a_1^{\mathsf T}x+b_1,\dots,a_L^{\mathsf T}x+b_L\}\) — a finite max of affine functions, hence convex.

Example 3.6 · Sum of the \(r\) largest componentsmax over C(n,r) sums

\(f(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{r}x_{[i]}\) (the \(r\) largest components) equals the maximum, over the \(\binom{n}{r}\) ways of choosing \(r\) distinct indices, of the sum of those components. Each such sum is linear in \(x\), so \(f\) is a max of linear functions — convex.

Example 3.7 · Support function of a set \(C\)sup of linear

\(S_C(x)=\sup\{x^{\mathsf T}y\mid y\in C\}\). For each fixed \(y\), \(x\mapsto x^{\mathsf T}y\) is linear in \(x\); the sup over \(y\in C\) is therefore convex — for any set \(C\) whatsoever, however wild.

Example 3.10 · Maximum eigenvalue of a symmetric matrixsup of linear in X

\(\lambda_{\max}(X)=\sup\{y^{\mathsf T}Xy\mid \lVert y\rVert_2=1\}\). Since \(y^{\mathsf T}Xy=\sum_{i,j}y_iX_{ij}y_j\) is linear in \(X\) for each fixed \(y\), \(\lambda_{\max}\) is a supremum of linear functions of \(X\) — convex.

Example 3.11 · Spectral (operator) norm of a matrixsup of linear in X

\(\lVert X\rVert_2=\sigma_{\max}(X)=\sup\{u^{\mathsf T}Xv\mid \lVert u\rVert_2=\lVert v\rVert_2=1\}\). Each \(u^{\mathsf T}Xv\) is linear in \(X\), so the spectral norm is a sup of linear functions of \(X\) — convex.

Example 3.9 · Least-squares cost as a function of weightsinf of linear → concave

\(g(w)=\inf_x\sum_i w_i(a_i^{\mathsf T}x-b_i)^2\). For each fixed \(x\), the objective is linear in \(w\); an infimum of linear (hence concave) functions is concave. This is the inf/concave side of the rule — note the curvature is concave, not convex.

§5representation as a pointwise sup of affine functions

A near-converse to the whole section: up to a technical condition, every convex function is the pointwise supremum of the affine functions that underestimate it. The method of §4 is not just one trick among many — it is, in a sense, the general shape of every convexity proof.

For \(f:\mathbf R^{n}\to\mathbf R\) convex with \(\operatorname{dom}f=\mathbf R^{n}\), \[ f(x)=\sup\{\,g(x)\mid g\ \text{affine},\ g(z)\le f(z)\ \forall z\,\}. \] That is, \(f\) equals the sup of all its affine (global) underestimators.B&V §3.2.3 · p. 83
f = sup of its affine underestimatorsa non-vertical supporting hyperplane
\[ \sup\{g(x)\mid g\ \text{affine},\ g\le f\}\ \le\ f(x) \]
(≥ is immediate.) Every admissible \(g\) satisfies \(g(x)\le f(x)\), so the sup cannot exceed \(f(x)\). The work is the reverse.
\[ a^{\mathsf T}(x-z)+b\,(f(x)-t)\le 0\quad\forall(z,t)\in\operatorname{epi}f \]
the crux. \(\operatorname{epi}f\) is convex, so it has a supporting hyperplane at the boundary point \((x,f(x))\), with normal \((a,b)\ne 0\).
\[ t=f(z)+s,\ s\ge 0\ \Rightarrow\ b\ge 0;\qquad b=0\Rightarrow a=0\ (\text{contradiction}) \]
letting \(s\to\infty\) forces \(b\ge 0\); if \(b=0\) the inequality forces \(a=0\), impossible. So \(b>0\): the hyperplane is non-vertical.
\[ g(z)=f(x)+(a/b)^{\mathsf T}(x-z)\ \le\ f(z),\qquad g(x)=f(x). \]
divide by \(b>0\). This \(g\) is affine, underestimates \(f\), and is tight at \(x\) — so the sup is attained. ∎
+

Why non-vertical matters. A vertical supporting hyperplane (\(b=0\)) would give no affine function of \(x\) — it constrains the domain, not the value. Convexity with \(\operatorname{dom}f=\mathbf R^n\) rules it out, which is exactly the "technical condition" that makes the representation clean.

§6expert lens — what to keep straight (diagnose)

D1Direction

sup / max of convex → convex; inf / min of concave → concave. Not the other curvature. Example 3.9 (least-squares in \(w\)) is the inf/concave trap.

D2Epigraph = intersection

epi g = ⋂ᵧ epi f(·,y) — intersection of convex sets. min fails because its epigraph is a union.

D3The method

Write \(f\) as a sup / max of affine or linear functions: support function, \(\lambda_{\max}\), \(\lVert X\rVert_2\), piecewise-linear, sum-of-\(r\)-largest.

D4Linear-in-X

\(y^{\mathsf T}Xy\) and \(u^{\mathsf T}Xv\) are linear in the matrix \(X\) — that is precisely why \(\lambda_{\max}\) and \(\lVert X\rVert_2\) are sups of linear functions.

D5Representation

A convex \(f\) (with \(\operatorname{dom}f=\mathbf R^n\)) equals the sup of its affine underestimators; the proof hinges on a non-vertical (\(b>0\)) supporting hyperplane to \(\operatorname{epi}f\).

Summary · 2026-07-20 · bv.3.2.3 · Pointwise maximum and supremum

Main points for quick review

The rule

Pointwise max / sup of convex is convex; inf of concave is concave.

Proof (max)

\(\max_i f_i(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le\max_i(\theta f_i(x)+(1-\theta)f_i(y))\le\theta\max_i f_i(x)+(1-\theta)\max_i f_i(y)\) — convexity + subadditivity of max + \(\theta,1-\theta\ge 0\).

Epigraph "why"

\(\operatorname{epi}g=\bigcap_y\operatorname{epi}f(\cdot,y)\); min ↔ union ↔ not convex.

The method

Write \(f\) as a sup / max of affine or linear: support function, \(\lambda_{\max}\), \(\lVert X\rVert_2\), piecewise-linear, sum-of-\(r\)-largest.

Representation

Convex \(f\) (\(\operatorname{dom}=\mathbf R^n\)) = sup of its affine underestimators, via a non-vertical (\(b>0\)) supporting hyperplane to \(\operatorname{epi}f\).
