the catalogue

The standard convex and concave functions to recognize on sight. Each earns its label by one of three verifications: the defining inequality (3.1) directly, a positive/negative semidefinite Hessian, or restriction to a line. Know the entries cold; reproduce log-sum-exp and log-det.

§0where we are

§3.1.1–3.1.4 gave the definition of convexity and the first- and second-order tests. §3.1.5 is the catalogue: the standard convex and concave functions worth recognizing instantly, each verified by one of three methods — the defining inequality (3.1) applied directly, the Hessian condition \(\nabla^2 f\succeq 0\) / \(\preceq 0\), or restriction of \(f\) to a line. The work here is memory plus a few reproducible proofs — above all log-sum-exp and log-det.

§1functions on ℝ

The one-variable catalogue. In each case the classification is read straight off the sign of \(f''\).

functiondomainconvex / concave
\(e^{ax}\)  (any \(a\))\(\mathbb{R}\)convex — \(f''=a^2e^{ax}\ge 0\)
\(x^{a}\)\(\mathbb{R}_{++}\)convex if \(a\ge 1\) or \(a\le 0\);  concave if \(0\le a\le 1\)
\(|x|^{p}\)  (\(p\ge 1\))\(\mathbb{R}\)convex
\(\log x\)\(\mathbb{R}_{++}\)concave — \(f''=-1/x^2\)
\(x\log x\)  (neg. entropy)\(\mathbb{R}_{++}\) (or \(\mathbb{R}_{+}\), \(0\log 0=0\))convex — \(f'=\log x+1,\ f''=1/x>0\)
!

The power rule is one computation. For \(f(x)=x^a\) on \(\mathbb{R}_{++}\), \(f''(x)=a(a-1)\,x^{a-2}\), and \(x^{a-2}>0\), so the sign of \(f''\) is exactly the sign of \(a(a-1)\): positive when \(a\ge 1\) or \(a\le 0\) (convex), negative when \(0\le a\le 1\) (concave). At \(a=0\) and \(a=1\) the function is affine — both.

Log-sum-exp is a smooth maximum

The name to keep: log-sum-exp is a differentiable approximation of \(\max\), sandwiched within \(\log n\) of it:

\[ \max\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\ \le\ \log\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i} e^{x_i}\Big)\ \le\ \max\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}+\log n. \]B&V §3.1.5 · L3639–3682
  • lower
    The sum is at least its largest term: \(\sum_i e^{x_i}\ge e^{\max_i x_i}\), so \(\log\sum_i e^{x_i}\ge \max_i x_i\).
  • upper
    The sum is at most \(n\) copies of the largest: \(\sum_i e^{x_i}\le n\,e^{\max_i x_i}\), so \(\log\sum_i e^{x_i}\le \max_i x_i+\log n\). The right bound is tight exactly when all \(x_i\) are equal.
  • three methods
    Everything in the catalogue is verified one of three ways: apply (3.1) directly (norms, max), show the Hessian is PSD / NSD (\(x^2/y\), lse, geometric mean), or restrict to a line (log-det).

§3direct (3.1) proofs — norms & max

Two catalogue entries need no calculus at all: the defining inequality (3.1) falls out of their algebra.

Every norm is convextriangle inequality + homogeneity
\[ f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)\ \le\ f(\theta x)+f\big((1-\theta)y\big) \]
triangle inequality of the norm applied to the two pieces
\[ =\ \theta\,f(x)+(1-\theta)\,f(y) \]
absolute homogeneity: \(f(\theta x)=\theta f(x)\) since \(\theta\ge 0\). This is exactly (3.1) — no differentiability used.
The max is convexmax of a sum ≤ sum of maxes
\[ f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)=\max_i\big(\theta x_i+(1-\theta)y_i\big) \]
definition of \(f=\max_i(\cdot)_i\) on the convex combination
\[ \le\ \theta\max_i x_i+(1-\theta)\max_i y_i=\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y) \]
max of a sum \(\le\) sum of maxes, and \(\theta,1-\theta\ge 0\). Again (3.1) directly.
§4hessian proofs — quad-over-linear & log-sum-exp

These two are verified by exhibiting the Hessian as a positive semidefinite form. Log-sum-exp is the one to be able to reproduce.

\(x^2/y\) is convex on \(\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}_{++}\)rank-one PSD Hessian
\[ \nabla^2 f(x,y)=\frac{2}{y^3}\begin{bmatrix} y \\ -x\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} y & -x\end{bmatrix}=\frac{2}{y^3}\begin{bmatrix} y^2 & -xy\\ -xy & x^2\end{bmatrix} \]
outer product of a vector with itself, scaled by \(2/y^3>0\)
\[ \Rightarrow\ \nabla^2 f\ \succeq\ 0 \]
any \(c\,vv^{\mathsf T}\) with \(c>0\) is PSD (its one nonzero eigenvalue is positive). Convex.
Log-sum-exp is convexCauchy–Schwarz on the Hessian form
\[ z=(e^{x_1},\dots,e^{x_n}),\quad \nabla^2 f=\frac{1}{(\mathbf 1^{\mathsf T}z)^2}\Big[(\mathbf 1^{\mathsf T}z)\,\mathrm{diag}(z)-zz^{\mathsf T}\Big] \]
differentiate \(f=\log\sum_i e^{x_i}\) twice; \(\mathbf 1^{\mathsf T}z=\sum_i z_i\)
\[ v^{\mathsf T}\nabla^2 f\,v=\frac{\big(\sum_i z_i\big)\big(\sum_i v_i^2 z_i\big)-\big(\sum_i v_i z_i\big)^2}{(\mathbf 1^{\mathsf T}z)^2}\ \ge\ 0 \]
Cauchy–Schwarz with \(a_i=v_i\sqrt{z_i},\ b_i=\sqrt{z_i}\): \((\sum a_i^2)(\sum b_i^2)\ge(\sum a_ib_i)^2\). Hence \(\succeq 0\), convex.
§5parallel proofs — geometric mean & log-det

The two concave headliners. The geometric mean is the sign-flipped twin of log-sum-exp — same Cauchy–Schwarz, opposite conclusion. Log-det is proved by restricting to a line.

Geometric mean is concave on \(\mathbb{R}^n_{++}\)Cauchy–Schwarz — the ⪯ 0 twin
\[ \nabla^2 f=-\frac{(\prod_i x_i)^{1/n}}{n^2}\Big[\,n\,\mathrm{diag}(1/x_i^2)-qq^{\mathsf T}\Big],\quad q_i=1/x_i \]
same shape as lse, with an overall minus sign
\[ v^{\mathsf T}\nabla^2 f\,v\ \le\ 0\quad\text{since}\quad n\sum_i \frac{v_i^2}{x_i^2}\ \ge\ \Big(\sum_i \frac{v_i}{x_i}\Big)^2 \]
Cauchy–Schwarz with \(a=\mathbf 1,\ b_i=v_i/x_i\). The bracket is \(\succeq 0\), so \(\nabla^2 f\preceq 0\). Concave.
Log-det is concave on \(\mathbf{S}^n_{++}\)restrict to a line
\[ X=Z+tV,\quad Z\succ 0,\qquad g(t)=\log\det(Z+tV) \]
take an arbitrary line through \(Z\succ 0\) in direction \(V\in\mathbf S^n\)
\[ g(t)=\log\det Z+\sum_{i=1}^n \log(1+t\lambda_i),\quad \lambda_i=\text{eig}\big(Z^{-1/2}VZ^{-1/2}\big) \]
factor \(Z^{1/2}(I+tZ^{-1/2}VZ^{-1/2})Z^{1/2}\); \(\det\) splits, and \(\det(I+tM)=\prod(1+t\lambda_i)\)
\[ g''(t)=-\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\lambda_i^2}{(1+t\lambda_i)^2}\ \le\ 0 \]
each term is a negative square. So \(g\) is concave on every line — hence \(\log\det X\) is concave.

§6worked examples
Example 1 — powers \(x^a\) for \(a=2,\tfrac12,-1,1\)sign of a(a−1)

On \(\mathbb{R}_{++}\), \(f''(x)=a(a-1)x^{a-2}\) with \(x^{a-2}>0\), so classification is the sign of \(a(a-1)\):

\[ a=2:\ a(a-1)=2>0\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{convex}\ (x^2). \qquad a=\tfrac12:\ a(a-1)=-\tfrac14<0\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{concave}\ (\sqrt{x}). \]

\[ a=-1:\ a(a-1)=2>0\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{convex}\ (1/x\ \text{on }x>0). \qquad a=1:\ a(a-1)=0\ \Rightarrow\ f''=0,\ \text{affine (both).} \]

The two boundaries \(a=0\) and \(a=1\) are precisely where curvature vanishes; between them (\(0\le a\le 1\)) the function is concave, outside them (\(a\ge 1\) or \(a\le 0\)) convex.

Example 2 — log-sum-exp with \(n=2\)max ≤ lse ≤ max + log 2

Take \(x=(1,2)\). Then \(\max=2\), and

\[ \mathrm{lse}(1,2)=\log(e^{1}+e^{2})=\log(2.71828+7.38906)=\log(10.1073)\approx 2.3133. \]

Check the sandwich with \(\log 2\approx 0.6931\): \[ 2\ \le\ 2.3133\ \le\ 2+0.6931=2.6931.\ \checkmark \]

The equal case \(x=(0,0)\) makes the upper bound tight: \(\mathrm{lse}(0,0)=\log(1+1)=\log 2\), while \(\max=0\), so \(\mathrm{lse}=\max+\log 2\) exactly. As the coordinates spread apart, the gap collapses toward the lower bound.

Example 3 — the \(x^2/y\) Hessian at \((1,2)\)rank-one, ⪰ 0

With \(\nabla^2 f=\dfrac{2}{y^3}\begin{bmatrix} y^2 & -xy\\ -xy & x^2\end{bmatrix}\), evaluate at \(x=1,\ y=2\) (so \(2/y^3=\tfrac14\)):

\[ \nabla^2 f(1,2)=\tfrac14\begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2\\ -2 & 1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5\\ -0.5 & 0.25\end{bmatrix}. \]

\(\det=1(0.25)-(-0.5)^2=0\) and \(\operatorname{tr}=1.25\), so the eigenvalues are \(\{1.25,\ 0\}\): positive semidefinite, singular (rank one). It is flat along \((x,y)=(1,2)\) — indeed \((y,-x)\cdot(x,y)=yx-xy=0\) — and positively curved in every other direction. Convex, not strictly.

Example 4 — log-det on the line \(Z=I,\ V=\mathrm{diag}(1,-1)\)g(t) = log(1−t²)

Since \(Z=I\), \(Z^{-1/2}VZ^{-1/2}=V=\mathrm{diag}(1,-1)\), so \(\lambda=(1,-1)\) and

\[ g(t)=\log\det(I+tV)=\log\big((1+t)(1-t)\big)=\log(1-t^2),\qquad -1

\[ g''(t)=-\Big[\frac{1}{(1+t)^2}+\frac{1}{(1-t)^2}\Big]<0, \]

matching \(g''=-\sum_i \lambda_i^2/(1+t\lambda_i)^2\). At \(t=0\), \(g''(0)=-2\). The curve is concave and dives to \(-\infty\) as \(t\to\pm 1\), where \(X=I+tV\) hits the boundary of \(\mathbf S^2_{++}\).

§7expert lens — diagnose

D1Know the catalogue cold

Convex: norms, \(\max\), \(x^2/y\), log-sum-exp, \(x\log x\), \(|x|^p\ (p\ge1)\), \(e^{ax}\). Concave: \(\log x\), geometric mean, \(\log\det X\). And \(x^a\): convex for \(a\ge1\) or \(a\le0\), concave for \(0\le a\le1\).

D2Cauchy–Schwarz is the engine

It powers both \(\log\sum e^{x_i}\) (\(\succeq 0\)) and the geometric mean (\(\preceq 0\)) — the same inequality, read with opposite sign.

D3Log-sum-exp = smooth max

\(\max\{x_i\}\le \log\sum_i e^{x_i}\le \max\{x_i\}+\log n\). The upper bound is tight when all \(x_i\) are equal.

D4Log-det via eigenvalues on a line

Restrict to \(Z+tV\); with \(\lambda_i=\text{eig}(Z^{-1/2}VZ^{-1/2})\), \(g''(t)=-\sum_i \lambda_i^2/(1+t\lambda_i)^2\le 0\). Concave.

D5Domains matter

The strictly-positive orthant for \(x^a,\ \log x,\ \text{geo mean}\); \(y>0\) for \(x^2/y\); \(X\succ 0\) for \(\log\det\). Drop the domain and the classification can fail.

D6Which method, and why

(3.1) directly for norms and \(\max\); Hessian + Cauchy–Schwarz for \(x^2/y\), lse, geometric mean; line restriction for \(\log\det\).

Main points for quick review

On ℝ

\(e^{ax}\) convex; \(x^a\) convex (\(a\ge1\) or \(a\le0\)) / concave (\(0\le a\le1\)); \(|x|^p\ (p\ge1)\) convex; \(\log x\) concave; \(x\log x\) convex.

On ℝⁿ

Norms, \(\max\), \(x^2/y\), log-sum-exp convex; geometric mean and \(\log\det X\) concave.

lse ≈ max

\(\max\{x_i\}\le \log\sum_i e^{x_i}\le \max\{x_i\}+\log n\).

Proof methods

(3.1) direct (norms, max); Hessian + Cauchy–Schwarz (\(x^2/y\), lse, geo mean); line restriction (log-det).

Watch domains

\(\mathbb{R}_{++}\) for powers/log/geo-mean; \(\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}_{++}\) for \(x^2/y\); \(\mathbf S^n_{++}\) for \(\log\det\).
