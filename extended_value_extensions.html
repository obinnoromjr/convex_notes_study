Extended-value
extensions

A notational device, not a new theorem: fold the domain into the function by sending it to \(+\infty\) off \(\operatorname{dom}f\). The chord inequality then holds for all \(x,y\) — the domain qualifiers vanish, sums acquire their domains for free, and a hard constraint dissolves into the objective.

§0where we are

§3.1.1 defined convex functions carrying the qualifier "for all \(x,y\in\operatorname{dom}f\)" plus the separate "\(\operatorname{dom}f\) convex" clause. §3.1.2 introduces a notational device — the extended-value extension — that folds the domain into the function so those qualifiers disappear. It is a convention, not a new theorem: it makes sums, the indicator trick, and constrained minimization cleaner to write.

§1the extension
For a convex \(f\), its extended-value extension \(\tilde f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}\) agrees with \(f\) on the domain and is \(+\infty\) off it: \[ \tilde f(x)=\begin{cases} f(x), & x\in\operatorname{dom}f,\\[2pt] \infty, & x\notin\operatorname{dom}f. \end{cases} \]B&V §3.1.2 · L3424–3432

\(\tilde f\) is defined on all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), and nothing on \(\operatorname{dom}f\) changes. The domain is recoverable from the extension alone:

\[ \operatorname{dom}f=\{\,x\mid \tilde f(x)<\infty\,\}. \]B&V §3.1.2 · L3430–3432
!

The extension carries the same information as the pair \((f,\operatorname{dom}f)\) — the finite part is \(f\), and the \(\infty\)-region is the complement of the domain. No data is lost; the bookkeeping just moves into the function's values.

§2dropping the domain qualifier

Extend the arithmetic to \(\infty\) with the natural conventions

\[ c\le\infty\ \ (\forall c\in\mathbb{R}),\qquad \infty+c=\infty,\qquad \theta\cdot\infty=\infty\ \ (\theta>0). \]B&V §3.1.2 · L3436–3446

Then the basic inequality (3.1), written for the extension, needs no domain qualifier at all:

for \(0<\theta<1\) and any \(x,y\in\mathbb{R}^n\), \[ \tilde f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)\ \le\ \theta\,\tilde f(x)+(1-\theta)\,\tilde f(y). \]B&V §3.1.2 · L3440–3446

This single inequality over all \(x,y\) carries (3.1) and the "\(\operatorname{dom}f\) convex" bookkeeping, checked case by case:

The "\(\operatorname{dom}f\) convex" clause is not thrown away — it is absorbed into the finite case. When both endpoints are in the domain and the RHS is finite, the inequality can only make sense if the interpolated point is in the domain too, which is precisely domain-convexity.

§3automatic domains — the sum

For a sum \(f=f_1+f_2\) of convex functions, the domain is the intersection \(\operatorname{dom}f=\operatorname{dom}f_1\cap\operatorname{dom}f_2\). With extensions this is automatic — just write

\[ \tilde f=\tilde f_1+\tilde f_2, \]B&V §3.1.2 · L3446–3459

because \(\infty+c=\infty\): any point outside either domain contributes an \(\infty\) and is thereby expelled from \(\operatorname{dom}f\). The intersection appears for free, with no separate side-condition to track.

!

Convention (symbol overload): B&V uses the same symbol for \(f\) and its extension \(\tilde f\). From here on convex functions are implicitly extended — taken to be \(\infty\) outside their domains — so an expression like \(f_1+f_2\) already means \(\tilde f_1+\tilde f_2\), intersection-of-domains included.

§4the indicator function
Example 3.1. For a convex set \(C\), the indicator function \(I_C\) has extension \[ \tilde I_C(x)=\begin{cases}0,& x\in C,\\[2pt]\infty,& x\notin C.\end{cases} \] \(\tilde I_C\) is convex (its "domain" is \(C\)).B&V §3.1.2 · Example 3.1 · L3461–3489

The indicator turns membership into a value: \(0\) if you are in \(C\), a prohibitive \(\infty\) if you are not. Its use is to absorb a hard constraint into the objective:

minimizing \(f\) over \(C\) is the same as minimizing \(f+\tilde I_C\) over all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), \[ \min_{x\in C} f(x)\ \equiv\ \min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n}\ \big(f(x)+\tilde I_C(x)\big), \] since \(f+\tilde I_C\) equals \(f\) on \(C\) and \(\infty\) elsewhere.B&V §3.1.2 · L3480–3489
!

Off \(C\) the sum is \(\infty\), so no minimizer can ever live there; on \(C\) it is just \(f\). A constrained problem becomes a single unconstrained minimization of one extended-value function.

§5worked examples
Example 1 — extend & recoverthe round trip

Let \(f(x)=1/x\) on \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x>0\}\) (convex). Its extension is

\[ \tilde f(x)=\begin{cases}1/x,&x>0,\\ \infty,&x\le0.\end{cases} \]

Recover the domain from the extension: \(\{x\mid \tilde f(x)<\infty\}=\{x>0\}=\operatorname{dom}f\). The finite region is \(f\); the \(\infty\)-region is the complement of the domain.

Example 2 — the sum's domain, computed[0,2] = intersection

Take \(f_1\) convex on \(\operatorname{dom}f_1=[0,3]\) and \(f_2\) convex on \(\operatorname{dom}f_2=[-1,2]\). Writing \(\tilde f=\tilde f_1+\tilde f_2\):

\[ \operatorname{dom}(f_1+f_2)=[0,3]\cap[-1,2]=[0,2]. \]

At \(x=2.5\in\operatorname{dom}f_1\) but \(\notin\operatorname{dom}f_2\), we get \(\tilde f_2(2.5)=\infty\), so \(\tilde f(2.5)=\tilde f_1(2.5)+\infty=\infty\) — expelled. The domain \([0,2]\) drops out with no extra work.

Example 3 — the unit-disk indicatorconstraint absorbed

Let \(C=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^2\mid \|x\|_2\le1\}\), the unit disk. Then

\[ \tilde I_C(x)=\begin{cases}0,&\|x\|_2\le1,\\ \infty,&\|x\|_2>1,\end{cases} \]

and \(\min_{\|x\|_2\le1} f(x)=\min_{x}\big(f(x)+\tilde I_C(x)\big)\). The disk constraint has become one term of the objective; no minimizer can sit outside the disk because the value there is \(\infty\).

Example 4 — the off-domain inequality is trivialnothing to prove

With \(f(x)=x^2\) on \(\operatorname{dom}f=[-1,1]\), test the extended inequality at \(x=0.5\in\operatorname{dom}f\), \(y=3\notin\operatorname{dom}f\), \(\theta=\tfrac12\):

\[ \theta\,\tilde f(x)+(1-\theta)\,\tilde f(y)=\tfrac12 f(0.5)+\tfrac12\cdot\infty=\infty, \]

so \(\tilde f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le\infty\) holds automatically — the single \(\infty\) endpoint makes the RHS \(\infty\) and the case is discharged without touching the LHS.

§6the concave mirror
A concave function is extended by \(-\infty\) off its domain: \[ \tilde f(x)=\begin{cases}f(x),&x\in\operatorname{dom}f,\\ -\infty,&x\notin\operatorname{dom}f,\end{cases} \] the mirror of the convex \(+\infty\).B&V §3.1.2 · L3490–3492

The sign matches everything else: \(-g\) is convex when \(g\) is concave, and the concave chord inequality is reversed (\(\ge\)), so the off-domain value that makes it hold trivially must be \(-\infty\), not \(+\infty\).

§7expert lens — diagnose

D1Extend & recover

f̃ = f on dom f, ∞ off it. Recover the domain any time via dom f = {x | f̃(x) < ∞} — no information is lost.

D2Qualifier drops

The \(\tilde f\)-form of (3.1) holds for all \(x,y\). Off-domain endpoint ⟹ RHS = ∞ ⟹ trivially true; this quietly folds in the "\(\operatorname{dom}f\) convex" clause.

D3Auto-domain

Write f̃₁ + f̃₂ and the domain dom f₁ ∩ dom f₂ appears for free — ∞ + c = ∞ expels any point outside either domain.

D4Indicator trick

Ĩ_C = 0 on \(C\), ∞ off. Then min over C of f ⟺ min over ℝⁿ of (f + Ĩ_C). A constraint becomes an objective term.

D5Concave sign

Concave functions extend by −∞, not +∞ — the mirror that keeps the reversed (\(\ge\)) inequality trivially satisfied off-domain.

D6It's a convention

Nothing here is a theorem. The extension is notation that makes definitions, sums, and constrained problems cleaner; convex functions are henceforth implicitly extended.

Main points for quick review

The extension

\(\tilde f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}\), \(=f\) on \(\operatorname{dom}f\), \(\infty\) off. Recover \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x\mid\tilde f(x)<\infty\}\).

Qualifier drops

\(\tilde f\)-form of (3.1) for \(0<\theta<1\), all \(x,y\). Off-domain endpoint ⟹ RHS \(\infty\) ⟹ trivially holds; drops the domain qualifier.

Sum

\(\tilde f_1+\tilde f_2\) ⟹ \(\operatorname{dom}=\operatorname{dom}f_1\cap\operatorname{dom}f_2\) automatically, via \(\infty+c=\infty\).

Indicator

\(\tilde I_C=0\) on \(C\), \(\infty\) off. \(\min f\) over \(C\) ⟺ \(\min(f+\tilde I_C)\) over \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Concave

Extend by \(-\infty\) — the mirror of the convex \(+\infty\).
