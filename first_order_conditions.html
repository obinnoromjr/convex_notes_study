First-order
conditions

When \(f\) is differentiable, convexity is a single gradient inequality: the tangent at any point — the first-order Taylor approximation — lies on or below the entire graph. Local data (value and slope at \(x\)) becomes a global lower bound. B&V calls this "perhaps the most important property of convex functions."

tangent = global underestimator f(y) ≥ f(x)+∇f(x)ᵀ(y−x) ∇f=0 ⟹ global min p. 69–70 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

§3.1.1 defined convexity by the chord inequality; §3.1.2 gave the extended-value notation. §3.1.3 adds the first calculus test: when \(f\) is differentiable, convexity is equivalent to a single gradient inequality. Its consequence — the first-order Taylor approximation being a global underestimator — is the property B&V singles out as "perhaps the most important" of convex functions, and this is the section whose proof is worth reproducing.

§1the first-order condition (3.2)

Suppose \(f\) is differentiable (its gradient \(\nabla f\) exists at each point of \(\operatorname{dom}f\), which is open). Then:

\(f\) is convex if and only if \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is convex and \[ f(y)\ \ge\ f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x) \] holds for all \(x,y\in\operatorname{dom}f\).B&V §3.1.3 · Eq. (3.2) · L3495–3499

Both parts are needed: an open, convex domain and the inequality for every ordered pair \((x,y)\). The right-hand side uses only local data at \(x\) — the value \(f(x)\) and the gradient \(\nabla f(x)\).

i

Read the inequality as anchored at \(x\) and tested against \(y\): fix the point of tangency \(x\), then \(y\) ranges over the whole domain. The affine function of \(y\) on the right is the tangent to the graph at \(x\).

§2the global underestimator

The right-hand side \(f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\) is exactly the first-order Taylor approximation of \(f\) near \(x\) — the tangent (hyper)plane at \(x\). Inequality (3.2) says that for a convex function this tangent lies on or below the entire graph:

From local information about \(f\) at a point \(x\) — its value and gradient — we obtain a global lower bound on \(f\): the affine function \(y\mapsto f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\) underestimates \(f\) everywhere.B&V §3.1.3 · L3500–3509

This "local implies global" passage is the heart of convexity's usefulness in optimization, and it runs both ways: if the tangent at every point underestimates \(f\), then \(f\) is convex. Convexity and "every tangent lies below the graph" are the same statement.

§3corollary — stationary ⟹ global minimizer

Put \(\nabla f(x)=0\) into (3.2). The linear term vanishes and the inequality collapses to:

If \(\nabla f(x)=0\), then \(f(y)\ge f(x)\) for all \(y\in\operatorname{dom}f\) — so \(x\) is a global minimizer of \(f\).B&V §3.1.3 · L3510–3512

For a differentiable convex \(f\), the first-order stationarity condition \(\nabla f=0\) is not merely necessary but sufficient for global optimality — one of the remarkable consequences B&V attributes to (3.2). A horizontal tangent, being a global underestimator that touches the graph at \(x\), pins the floor of \(f\) at \(x\).

§4strict & concave variants
  • strict
    \(f\) is strictly convex iff \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is convex and \[ f(y)\ >\ f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\quad\text{for all } x\ne y. \] The tangent touches only at \(x\) and lies strictly below the graph everywhere else.
  • concave
    \(f\) is concave iff the inequality reverses: \[ f(y)\ \le\ f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x). \] The tangent now lies on or above the graph — a global overestimator.
L3513–3522 — strict convexity sharpens \(\ge\) to \(>\) off the diagonal; concavity is the mirror image with \(\le\).B&V §3.1.3 · L3513–3522
§5proof — the drill

The proof reduces the general case to one dimension via the line-restriction of §3.1.1: \(f\) is convex iff it is convex along every line. So first prove the scalar version, then lift it.

Step A — the scalar case (\(n=1\))

Claim: for \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\), \(f\) is convex \(\iff f(y)\ge f(x)+f'(x)(y-x)\).

Necessity — convex ⟹ the inequalitydifference quotient, t→0⁺
\[ f\big(x+t(y-x)\big)\ \le\ (1-t)f(x)+t\,f(y) \]
convexity of \(f\) applied to the point \(x+t(y-x)\), for \(0
\[ \frac{f\big(x+t(y-x)\big)-f(x)}{t}\ \le\ f(y)-f(x) \]
subtract \(f(x)\), divide by \(t>0\) — rearranges the chord bound into a difference quotient
\[ t\to 0^{+}:\quad f'(x)(y-x)\ \le\ f(y)-f(x) \]
the left side \(\to f'(x)(y-x)\) by the definition of the derivative ⟹ the inequality (3.2)
Sufficiency — the inequality ⟹ convexapply twice about z; f′ cancels
\[ z=\theta x+(1-\theta)y,\qquad 0\le\theta\le 1 \]
pick any two points \(x,y\) and an interpolate \(z\) between them
\[ f(x)\ge f(z)+f'(z)(x-z),\qquad f(y)\ge f(z)+f'(z)(y-z) \]
apply the hypothesis twice, once with base point \(z\) and target \(x\), once with target \(y\)
\[ \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)\ \ge\ f(z)+f'(z)\underbrace{\big[\theta(x-z)+(1-\theta)(y-z)\big]}_{=\,0} \]
multiply by \(\theta\) and \(1-\theta\), add; the bracket is \(\theta x+(1-\theta)y-z=0\)
\[ \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)\ \ge\ f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big) \]
the \(f'(z)\) term vanishes ⟹ the chord inequality (3.1) — \(f\) is convex

Step B — general \(n\) via line restriction

For \(x,y\in\mathbb{R}^n\) define the scalar restriction \(g(t)=f\big(ty+(1-t)x\big)\), so that

\[ g'(t)=\nabla f\big(ty+(1-t)x\big)^{\mathsf T}(y-x),\quad g(0)=f(x),\ g(1)=f(y),\ g'(0)=\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x). \]B&V §3.1.3 · L3523–3555
Both directions — lift the scalar resultconvex ⟺ convex on every line
\[ f\ \text{convex}\ \Longrightarrow\ g\ \text{convex}\ \Longrightarrow\ g(1)\ge g(0)+g'(0) \]
necessity: each restriction \(g\) is convex (§3.1.1); Step A gives \(g(1)\ge g(0)+g'(0)\), which is exactly (3.2)
\[ (3.2)\ \forall x,y\ \Longrightarrow\ \text{every }g\text{ satisfies the scalar condition}\ \Longrightarrow\ g\ \text{convex} \]
sufficiency: (3.2) on all pairs forces the scalar inequality along every line, so every \(g\) is convex ⟹ \(f\) convex
\[ f\ \text{convex}\iff f\big|_{\text{line}}\ \text{convex on every line} \]
the §3.1.1 criterion — one line's convexity is not enough; sufficiency needs every line
!

Where the hypothesis is used: sufficiency requires (3.2) for all \(x,y\), not one pair. A single line's convexity says nothing about \(f\); only the full family of line restrictions recovers convexity of \(f\).

§6worked examples
Example 1 — the tangent gap for x²gap = (y − x)²

Let \(f(x)=x^2\), \(f'(x)=2x\). The tangent at \(x\) is \(f(x)+f'(x)(y-x)=x^2+2x(y-x)\). The gap is

\[ f(y)-\big[x^2+2x(y-x)\big]=y^2-2xy+x^2=(y-x)^2\ \ge\ 0, \]

with equality only at \(y=x\). The nonnegative gap \((y-x)^2\) is the underestimator inequality made explicit — and its strictness for \(y\ne x\) shows \(x^2\) is strictly convex.

Example 2 — the tangent to eˣ at 0eʸ ≥ 1 + y

Let \(f(y)=e^{y}\), convex, with \(f(0)=1\), \(f'(0)=1\). Anchoring (3.2) at \(x=0\):

\[ e^{y}\ \ge\ e^{0}+e^{0}(y-0)=1+y\quad\text{for all }y. \]

The famous bound \(e^{y}\ge 1+y\) is just "tangent below the graph" at a single point — the convex function's tangent line at the origin.

Example 3 — the tangent gap for ‖x‖²gap = ‖y − x‖²

Let \(f(x)=\|x\|_2^2=x^{\mathsf T}x\) on \(\mathbb{R}^n\), so \(\nabla f(x)=2x\). The gap is

\[ \|y\|^2-\big[\|x\|^2+2x^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\big]=\|y\|^2-2x^{\mathsf T}y+\|x\|^2=\|y-x\|^2\ \ge\ 0. \]

The multivariate mirror of Example 1: the underestimator gap is the squared distance \(\|y-x\|^2\), zero only at \(y=x\) — again strict convexity.

Example 4 — a stationary point is the global minx² − 2x at x = 1

Let \(f(x)=x^2-2x\), \(f'(x)=2x-2\). Setting \(f'(x)=0\) gives \(x=1\). Because \(f\) is convex, (3.2) with \(\nabla f(1)=0\) yields

\[ f(y)\ \ge\ f(1)+0\cdot(y-1)=f(1)=-1\quad\text{for all }y, \]

so \(x=1\) is the global minimizer — no second-order test needed. Stationarity plus convexity settles global optimality outright.

§7expert lens — diagnose

D1The condition (3.2)

\(f\) differentiable, \(\operatorname{dom}f\) open & convex: convex \(\iff f(y)\ge f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\). Direction \(\ge\) means the tangent sits below the graph.

D2Global underestimator

The RHS is the first-order Taylor approximation at \(x\). Local value + gradient ⟹ a global lower bound. The converse holds — this characterizes convexity.

D3Stationary ⟹ global min

\(\nabla f(x)=0\) in (3.2) gives \(f(y)\ge f(x)\ \forall y\). For differentiable convex \(f\), \(\nabla f=0\) is sufficient for global optimality — the payoff.

D4Strict & concave

Strict convexity: \(>\) for \(x\ne y\). Concave: reverse to \(\le\) (tangent above graph). Same picture, sharpened or mirrored.

D5Proof shape

Scalar case first (difference quotient + \(t\to0\); apply-twice about \(z\)), then general \(n\) by restricting to a line. Sufficiency needs convexity on every line, not one.

Main points for quick review

The condition

\(f\) differentiable: convex \(\iff \operatorname{dom}f\) convex and \(f(y)\ge f(x)+\nabla f(x)^{\mathsf T}(y-x)\) for all \(x,y\).

Reading

The tangent (first-order Taylor) is a global underestimator; local information yields a global bound.

Corollary

\(\nabla f(x)=0\ \Longrightarrow\ x\) is a global minimizer.

Variants

Strict: \(>\) for \(x\ne y\). Concave: \(\le\) (tangent above the graph).

Proof

\(n=1\) (difference quotient, \(t\to0\); apply-twice), then general \(n\) by line restriction — sufficiency needs every line.
