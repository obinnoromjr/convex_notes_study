Inequalities from convexity

The method is the content: a famous inequality is just Jensen applied to a well-chosen convex function. Pick −log x and AM–GM falls out; feed weighted AM–GM the right substitution and Hölder follows.

−log x convex — the chosen function Jensen — the engine AM–GM · Hölder p. 78–79 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

§3.1.8 gave Jensen's inequality in its general forms. §3.1.9 puts it to work. Boyd & Vandenberghe make the striking claim that convexity together with Jensen can be the foundation of a whole theory of inequalities: many famous inequalities are nothing more than Jensen applied to a well-chosen convex function. Two worked examples carry the section — AM–GM, which is easy, and Hölder, which is less trivial. The mastery target here is to diagnose: recognise the method, and reproduce the load-bearing steps of both derivations.

§1the method, and two-term AM–GM (3.6)

The recipe never changes: pick the right convex function, feed a convex combination into Jensen, and a famous inequality drops out. The first move — naming the function — is the whole trick.

\[ \sqrt{ab}\ \le\ \frac{a+b}{2}, \qquad a,b\ge 0. \tag{3.6} \]B&V §3.1.9 · (3.6) · arithmetic–geometric mean
Derivation of (3.6)Jensen on −log x at θ = 1/2
\[ -\log\!\Big(\tfrac{a+b}{2}\Big)\ \le\ \tfrac12(-\log a)+\tfrac12(-\log b) \]
−log x is convex. Jensen with \(\theta=\tfrac12\). The choice of function is the entire idea.
\[ -\log\!\Big(\tfrac{a+b}{2}\Big)\ \le\ -\log\!\big(\sqrt{ab}\big) \]
the right side is \(-\tfrac12(\log a+\log b)=-\log\sqrt{ab}\)
\[ \log\!\Big(\tfrac{a+b}{2}\Big)\ \ge\ \log\!\big(\sqrt{ab}\big) \]
multiply by \(-1\) — this flips \(\le\) to \(\ge\)
\[ \tfrac{a+b}{2}\ \ge\ \sqrt{ab} \]
exponentiate; \(\exp\) is increasing, so it preserves the direction. ∎
!

Mind the domain. The Jensen/\(-\log\) argument only runs for \(a,b>0\), where \(\log\) is defined. The inequality itself holds on all of \(a,b\ge0\): the boundary case \(a=0\) reads \(\sqrt{0}=0\le b/2\), which is trivially true. So the honest statement of the proof is: prove it for \(a,b>0\) by Jensen, then extend to the boundary by inspection.

§2the engine — general weighted AM–GM

Keep the same function \(-\log x\); run Jensen at a general weight \(\theta\) instead of \(\tfrac12\). This is not a new idea — it is the same picture with the midpoint slid — but it is the engine that will drive Hölder.

\[ a^{\theta}\,b^{\,1-\theta}\ \le\ \theta a+(1-\theta)b, \qquad a,b\ge 0,\ \ 0\le\theta\le 1. \]B&V §3.1.9 · weighted arithmetic–geometric mean
DerivationJensen on −log x at general θ
\[ -\log\!\big(\theta a+(1-\theta)b\big)\ \le\ \theta(-\log a)+(1-\theta)(-\log b) \]
Jensen on the convex \(-\log x\) at weight \(\theta\)
\[ \theta(-\log a)+(1-\theta)(-\log b)=-\log\!\big(a^{\theta}b^{\,1-\theta}\big) \]
a weighted sum of logs is the log of a product of powers
\[ a^{\theta}b^{\,1-\theta}\ \le\ \theta a+(1-\theta)b \]
multiply by \(-1\) (flips), then exponentiate (preserves). \(\theta=\tfrac12\) recovers (3.6). ∎
!

Why exponentiating is the crux. Jensen hands you an additive bound on \(-\log\). Because \(\theta\log a+(1-\theta)\log b=\log\!\big(a^{\theta}b^{\,1-\theta}\big)\), exponentiating turns that sum of logs into the multiplicative object \(a^{\theta}b^{\,1-\theta}\). That additive-to-multiplicative trade is the entire mechanism — and it is exactly why \(-\log\), whose defining feature is turning products into sums, is the function to pick.

§3Hölder's inequality

Now the less-trivial payoff. For \(p>1\), with \(\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1\), and any \(x,y\in\mathbf{R}^n\):

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i y_i\ \le\ \Big(\sum_{i=1}^{n}|x_i|^p\Big)^{1/p}\Big(\sum_{i=1}^{n}|y_i|^q\Big)^{1/q}. \]B&V §3.1.9 · Hölder's inequality

Here \(p\) and \(q\) are conjugate exponents: \(\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1\), equivalently \(1-\tfrac1p=\tfrac1q\). That single relation gets used in two different guises below — lose it and the final step breaks. (Take \(x,y\ne0\); if either vanishes the right side is \(0\) and the inequality is trivial. This also keeps the normalizing denominators positive.)

Derivation (B&V's own)weighted AM–GM, then sum
\[ a=\frac{|x_i|^p}{\sum_j|x_j|^p},\quad b=\frac{|y_i|^q}{\sum_j|y_j|^q},\quad \theta=\tfrac1p\ \ (1-\theta=\tfrac1q) \]
the substitution. Conjugacy enters first as \(1-\theta=\tfrac1q\)
\[ a^{\theta}b^{\,1-\theta}=\frac{|x_i|\,|y_i|}{\big(\sum_j|x_j|^p\big)^{1/p}\big(\sum_j|y_j|^q\big)^{1/q}} \]
exponent bookkeeping: \((|x_i|^p)^{1/p}=|x_i|\), \((|y_i|^q)^{1/q}=|y_i|\)
\[ \frac{|x_i|\,|y_i|}{(\sum_j|x_j|^p)^{1/p}(\sum_j|y_j|^q)^{1/q}}\ \le\ \tfrac1p\frac{|x_i|^p}{\sum_j|x_j|^p}+\tfrac1q\frac{|y_i|^q}{\sum_j|y_j|^q} \]
weighted AM–GM bounds \(a^{\theta}b^{1-\theta}\le\theta a+(1-\theta)b\)
\[ \sum_i\Big(\tfrac1p\tfrac{|x_i|^p}{\sum_j|x_j|^p}+\tfrac1q\tfrac{|y_i|^q}{\sum_j|y_j|^q}\Big)=\tfrac1p\cdot1+\tfrac1q\cdot1=\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1 \]
sum over \(i\): each normalized block sums to \(1\); conjugacy collapses the RHS to 1
\[ \sum_i|x_i|\,|y_i|\ \le\ \Big(\sum_i|x_i|^p\Big)^{1/p}\Big(\sum_i|y_i|^q\Big)^{1/q} \]
clear the (positive) denominator; then \(\sum_i x_iy_i\le\sum_i|x_i||y_i|\) gives Hölder. ∎
!

Conjugacy did the work twice. Once as \(1-\theta=\tfrac1q\) in the exponent step — that is what makes \((|y_i|^q)^{1/q}=|y_i|\) come out clean — and again at the end, where \(\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1\) is exactly what collapses the summed right-hand side to \(1\). Drop the conjugate relation and neither step lands.

§4conjugate exponents — where the RHS collapses to 1

The whole Hölder ending hinges on \(\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1\). It is worth seeing that relation on its own: two conjugate exponents partition a single unit of mass, and that partition is precisely the \(\tfrac1p\cdot1+\tfrac1q\cdot1\) that closes the proof.

Slide p > 1; the conjugate q = p/(p−1) is forced. The unit bar is split into 1/p (blue) and 1/q (vermilion) — they always tile exactly one unit, because 1/p + 1/q = 1. That is the same unit the summed Hölder RHS collapses to. At p = q = 2 the split is even and Hölder becomes Cauchy–Schwarz.

§5expert lens — what to keep straight (diagnose)

D1The method IS the content

Famous inequality = Jensen on the right convex function. For AM–GM (both forms) that function is −log x. Naming the function is the first move — everything after is bookkeeping.

D2Why −log, why exponentiate

Jensen gives an additive bound on −log; exp (monotone) turns it into a multiplicative statement. The ×(−1) reverses ≤; exp preserves it. Track both directions.

D3Weighted AM–GM is the engine

aᶿb¹⁻ᶿ ≤ θa+(1−θ)b is not a separate idea — same −log, general θ. θ=½ recovers (3.6); θ=1/p drives Hölder.

D4Hölder's substitution

a=|xᵢ|ᵖ/Σ|xⱼ|ᵖ, b=|yᵢ|^q/Σ|yⱼ|^q, θ=1/p. Each normalized block sums to 1; conjugacy enters twice — as 1−θ=1/q in the exponent step, and to collapse the summed RHS to 1.

D5Hypotheses

AM–GM on a,b ≥ 0 (proof runs for a,b>0, boundary by inspection). Hölder needs p>1 and 1/p+1/q=1. Drop conjugacy and the final step breaks.

Main points for quick review

The method

Famous inequality = Jensen on a well-chosen convex function. Convexity + Jensen can found a whole theory of inequalities.

AM–GM (3.6)

\(\sqrt{ab}\le\tfrac{a+b}{2}\): \(-\log\) convex + Jensen at \(\theta=\tfrac12\); then \(\times(-1)\) (flips) and exponentiate (preserves).

Weighted AM–GM

\(a^{\theta}b^{1-\theta}\le\theta a+(1-\theta)b\): same \(-\log\), general \(\theta\). The engine for Hölder.

Hölder

\(\sum x_iy_i\le(\sum|x_i|^p)^{1/p}(\sum|y_i|^q)^{1/q}\), \(p>1\), \(\tfrac1p+\tfrac1q=1\): weighted AM–GM with \(\theta=\tfrac1p\), then sum — conjugacy collapses the RHS to \(1\).
