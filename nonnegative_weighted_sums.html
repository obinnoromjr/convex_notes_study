Nonnegative weighted sums

The first closure rule in the "operations that preserve convexity" toolkit. Scale by α ≥ 0, then add — and convexity survives. Structurally: the convex functions form a convex cone.

f₁, f₂ — convex ingredients w₁f₁ + w₂f₂ — the sum (wᵢ ≥ 0) convex cone: closed under +, scaling p. 79 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

§3.2 opens the operations that preserve convexity toolkit — ways to build convex functions rather than re-checking the defining inequality (3.1) from scratch each time. §3.2.1 is the first and simplest closure property: nonnegative scaling and addition. The punchline is structural — the convex functions form a convex cone.

§1the two generators — scaling and addition

Two atomic moves, each a one-liner from the definition (3.1). Everything in this section is these two, combined.

Nonnegative scaling. If \(f\) is convex and \(\alpha\ge 0\), then \(\alpha f\) is convex. (For \(\alpha<0\), \(\alpha f\) is concave.)B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79
Why αf is convexα ≥ 0 preserves ≤
\[ (\alpha f)\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)=\alpha\, f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big) \]
definition of \(\alpha f\), then apply (3.1) to \(f\)
\[ \le\ \alpha\big(\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)\big) \]
the load-bearing step — multiplying an inequality by \(\alpha\) keeps \(\le\) only because \(\alpha\ge 0\). At \(\alpha<0\) it flips.
\[ =\ \theta(\alpha f)(x)+(1-\theta)(\alpha f)(y). \]
regroup — this is (3.1) for \(\alpha f\). ∎
Addition. If \(f_1,f_2\) are convex, so is \(f_1+f_2\).B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79

Add the two (3.1) inequalities for \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) term by term — the sum of the left sides is at most the sum of the right sides. Nothing more is needed.

§2nonnegative weighted sums — the convex cone

Combine the two generators and iterate: any nonnegative weighted sum of convex functions is convex.

If \(f_1,\dots,f_m\) are convex and \(w_1,\dots,w_m\ge 0\), then \[ f=w_1 f_1+\cdots+w_m f_m \] is convex. Equivalently, the set of convex functions is a convex cone. (The concave analogue holds too.)B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79
!

The \(w_i\ge 0\) is load-bearing, not bookkeeping. A negative weight turns that term concave, and a convex-plus-concave sum need not be convex. In particular f₁ − f₂ is not guaranteed convex, even when both \(f_1,f_2\) are. Nonnegativity is exactly what makes the cone a cone.

Strict version — mind the qualifier

A nonnegative, nonzero weighted sum of strictly convex (concave) functions is strictly convex (concave).B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79

"Nonzero" means at least one weight is strictly positive. The reason is exact: for \(x\ne y\), \(\theta\in(0,1)\), each term with \(w_i>0\) contributes a strict inequality while each \(w_i=0\) term contributes \(0=0\). A single positive weight is enough to make the whole sum strict. But if every weight is \(0\), then \(f\equiv 0\) — convex, yet not strictly. Hence the qualifier "nonzero."

§3infinite sums and integrals

The same principle carries from a finite sum to a continuum, provided the object on the right exists.

If \(f(x,y)\) is convex in \(x\) for each \(y\in A\), and \(w(y)\ge 0\) for each \(y\in A\), then \[ g(x)=\int_A w(y)\,f(x,y)\,dy \] is convex in \(x\) (provided the integral exists).B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79
+

Read the density \(w(y)\) as a continuum of nonnegative weights, one per \(y\). A finite nonnegative weighted sum is the special case where \(w\) is supported on finitely many points. Same theorem, same hypothesis — w(y) ≥ 0 — one level of generality up.

§4the epigraph view — the structural "why"

§3.1.7 gave the bridge: \(f\) convex \(\iff\) \(\operatorname{epi}f\) convex. That bridge turns the analytic (3.1) check for \(wf\) into a statement about a linear image of a convex set. For \(w>0\):

\[ \operatorname{epi}(wf)=\begin{bmatrix} I & 0\\ 0 & w\end{bmatrix}\operatorname{epi}f. \]B&V §3.2.1 · p. 79
epi(wf) is a linear image of epi f (w > 0)convex sets survive linear maps
\[ (x,t)\in\operatorname{epi}f \iff f(x)\le t \]
definition of the epigraph
\[ (x,t)\mapsto(x,wt) \]
the map. Its image is \(\{(x,wt)\mid f(x)\le t\}\)
\[ \{(x,wt)\mid f(x)\le t\}=\{(x,s)\mid wf(x)\le s\}=\operatorname{epi}(wf) \]
substitute \(s=wt\) (valid since \(w>0\))
\[ \Rightarrow\ \operatorname{epi}(wf)\text{ convex}\ \Rightarrow\ wf\text{ convex} \]
linear image of the convex set \(\operatorname{epi}f\) is convex (§2.3.2). ∎
!

The \(w=0\) caveat. At \(w=0\) the map \((x,t)\mapsto(x,0)\) collapses the vertical axis; its image \(\{(x,0)\mid x\in\operatorname{dom}f\}\) is not \(\operatorname{epi}(0\cdot f)=\operatorname{dom}f\times\mathbf{R}_+\). So the matrix identity is for \(w>0\); the \(w=0\) case is just \(0\cdot f=0\), trivially convex.

§5expert lens — what to keep straight (diagnose)

D1Headline

Nonnegative weighted sums preserve convexity — the convex functions form a convex cone. Scaling by α ≥ 0 and adding are the two generators; everything else is iteration.

D2wᵢ ≥ 0 is load-bearing

A negative weight makes that term concave and the sum need not be convex. f₁ − f₂ is not guaranteed convex. Reserve the cone for nonnegative weights.

D3"Nonzero" in the strict version

A nonneg nonzero weighted sum of strictly convex functions is strictly convex — needs ≥ one wᵢ > 0. All-zero gives f ≡ 0: convex, but not strict.

D4Epigraph reasoning

epi(wf) is a linear image of epi f (for w > 0), convex because linear images preserve convexity — the structural "why" behind the (3.1) checks. Mind the w = 0 degeneracy.

D5Scope — don't over-reach

This is only nonnegative sums / scaling. Pointwise max (§3.2.3), affine composition (§3.2.2), general composition (§3.2.4) are separate rules. max{f₁,f₂} is convex — but by a different theorem, not this one.

Main points for quick review

The generators

\(\alpha f\) convex for \(\alpha\ge0\) (\(\alpha<0\Rightarrow\) concave); \(f_1+f_2\) convex — so convex functions form a convex cone.

Nonneg weighted sum

\(\sum w_i f_i\) convex when \(w_i\ge0\). The \(w_i\ge0\) is essential — \(f_1-f_2\) is not guaranteed convex.

Strict needs "nonzero"

Strictly convex iff \(\ge\) one \(w_i>0\); all-zero \(\Rightarrow f\equiv0\), convex but not strict.

Integrals

\(g(x)=\int_A w(y)f(x,y)\,dy\) convex if \(f(\cdot,y)\) convex and \(w(y)\ge0\).

Epigraph "why"

\(\operatorname{epi}(wf)=\left[\begin{smallmatrix}I&0\\0&w\end{smallmatrix}\right]\operatorname{epi}f\) for \(w>0\) — a linear image of \(\operatorname{epi}f\), hence convex; \(w=0\) gives \(0\cdot f=0\) trivially.
