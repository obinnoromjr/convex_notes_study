General composition

The fourth closure rule, and the subtle one. Feed one function into another — f = h∘g — and four rules decide the curvature. The load-bearing detail: the monotonicity is on the extended-value extension h̃, not on h.

g — the inner function h̃ — outer, extended-value f = h∘g — inherits h's curvature p. 83–87 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

This is the fourth convexity-preserving operation and the most subtle: general composition \(f=h\circ g\), \(f(x)=h(g(x))\), with \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x\in\operatorname{dom}g\mid g(x)\in\operatorname{dom}h\}\). The rules form a small table, but the load-bearing detail is the monotonicity of the extended-value extension \(\tilde h\) — get that wrong (Remark 3.3) and the whole thing collapses.

§1scalar case — the second-derivative "why"

Take \(k=1\), everything twice differentiable, domains all of \(\mathbf R\). The chain rule gives \(f''\) directly, and reading its sign gives the four rules.

\[ f''(x)=h''\big(g(x)\big)\,g'(x)^2+h'\big(g(x)\big)\,g''(x). \tag{3.9}\] Two terms: \(h''g'^2\) carries \(h\)'s curvature (the \(g'^2\ge0\) factor can't flip it), and \(h'g''\) couples \(h\)'s monotonicity to \(g\)'s curvature.B&V §3.2.4 · p. 83, eq. 3.9
outer hinner gresult
1convex, nondecreasingconvexf convex
2convex, nonincreasingconcavef convex
3concave, nondecreasingconcavef concave
4concave, nonincreasingconvexf concave

Row 2 as a worked sign-read: \(h''g'^2\ge0\) (h convex), and \(h'g''=(\le0)(\le0)\ge0\) (h nonincreasing, g concave), so \(f''\ge0\) — convex. The pattern to keep: \(f\) inherits \(h\)'s curvature; \(g\) must match \(h\)'s monotonicity.

+

These are the four guaranteed pairings (eq. 3.10). The backwards pairing — convex nondecreasing \(h\) with a concave \(g\), say — gets no guarantee, and generally fails.

§2general rules (3.11) — the extended-value extension h̃

The same four rules hold with no differentiability — provided the monotonicity condition is read on the extended-value extension \(\tilde h\): \(h\) extended by \(+\infty\) off \(\operatorname{dom}h\) when \(h\) is convex, by \(-\infty\) when \(h\) is concave.

"\(\tilde h\) nondecreasing" is two conditions, both needed: (i) \(h\) is monotone on \(\operatorname{dom}h\), and (ii) \(\operatorname{dom}h\) extends the right way — and the direction depends on the curvature.B&V §3.2.4 · p. 83–84, eq. 3.11
hh̃ (via ±∞)dom h must extend
convex (+∞ off)nondecreasingleft
convexnonincreasingright
concave (−∞ off)nondecreasingright  (e.g. log on R₊₊)
concavenonincreasingleft

For convex \(h\), the \(+\infty\) extension jumps up off the domain, so "\(\tilde h\) nondecreasing" forces the domain to extend leftward (an \(+\infty\) sitting on the right is fine; on the left it would break monotonicity). Fig. 3.7 is the caution: \(x^2\) on \(\mathbf R_+\) is nondecreasing there, but its extension \(\tilde h\) — which is \(+\infty\) for \(x<0\) — is not nondecreasing. For concave \(h\) the \(-\infty\) extension flips every direction.

Remark 3.3 — why h̃, not just h

u = g(x) = x² band dom h = [a, b] dom f on the x-axis
g(x)=x², h ≡ 0 on dom h=[a,b]

Boyd's counterexample. \(h\) is constant on its domain, so it is nondecreasing there — yet \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x\mid x^2\in[a,b]\}\) can be two disjoint intervals, so \(f\) is not convex. Slide the left end a leftward (toward \(-\infty\)): the band drops below the vertex, \(\operatorname{dom}f\) fuses into one interval, and \(\tilde h\) becomes nondecreasing. The condition has to be on \(\tilde h\).

§3Example 3.12 — the h̃ condition in the wild

Four quick reads on \(x\mapsto\) a power/log, each testing the extension condition:

  • ✓
    \(\log x\) on \(\mathbf R_{++}\): concave, and \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing (domain extends right, \(-\infty\) fills the left) — so \(\log g\) is concave for concave positive \(g\).
  • ✓
    \(x^{1/2}\) on \(\mathbf R_+\): concave, \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing.
  • ✗
    \(x^{3/2}\) on \(\mathbf R_+\): convex, but \(\tilde h\) is not nondecreasing — \(\tilde h(-1)=+\infty>\tilde h(1)=1\). The condition fails as stated.
  • fix
    Clipped \(x^{3/2}\) (set \(=0\) for \(x<0\)) on all of \(\mathbf R\): now convex and \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing — the domain was extended leftward to repair it.
§4proof of the base case — no differentiability

Claim: \(g\) convex, \(h\) convex, \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing \(\Rightarrow f=h\circ g\) convex. Take \(x,y\in\operatorname{dom}f\), \(\theta\in[0,1]\).

h∘g convex from the definitionh̃ nondecreasing does two jobs
\[ g(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\ \le\ \theta g(x)+(1-\theta)g(y) \tag{3.12}\]
g convex. The inner inequality — the only place \(g\)'s convexity is used.
\[ \theta x+(1-\theta)y\in\operatorname{dom}f \]
domain job. RHS of (3.12) \(\in\operatorname{dom}h\); \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing (domain extends left) forces the smaller LHS into \(\operatorname{dom}h\) too — so \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is convex.
\[ h\big(g(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\big)\ \le\ h\big(\theta g(x)+(1-\theta)g(y)\big) \tag{3.13}\]
inequality job. apply the nondecreasing \(\tilde h\) to (3.12).
\[ \le\ \theta\,h(g(x))+(1-\theta)\,h(g(y))=\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y) \tag{3.14}\]
h convex. chain the three steps ⟹ \(f\) convex. ∎
!

Division of labor. \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing does two jobs — domain convexity and step (3.13); \(h\) convex does (3.14); \(g\) convex does (3.12). Every hypothesis is load-bearing, and that is exactly why weakening "\(\tilde h\)" to "\(h\)" (Remark 3.3) breaks the proof at the domain step.

§5Example 3.13 — everyday consequences (reach for these)

The five compositions you actually invoke, each an instance of a single rule:

  • exp
    \(g\) convex \(\Rightarrow \exp g\) convex.
  • log
    \(g\) concave and positive \(\Rightarrow \log g\) concave.
  • 1/g
    \(g\) concave and positive \(\Rightarrow 1/g\) convex.
  • gᵖ
    \(g\) convex and nonnegative, \(p\ge1 \Rightarrow g^{p}\) convex.
  • −log
    \(g\) convex \(\Rightarrow -\log(-g)\) convex on \(\{x\mid g(x)<0\}\).

§6Remark 3.3 — the cautionary tale, stated

Why the condition must be on \(\tilde h\) and not \(h\), in one clean counterexample (Lab 2 above is this remark, made draggable):

Take \(g(x)=x^2\) (convex) and \(h=0\) on \(\operatorname{dom}h=[1,2]\) (convex, and nondecreasing on its domain). Then \[ \operatorname{dom}f=\{x\mid x^2\in[1,2]\}=[-\sqrt2,-1]\cup[1,\sqrt2] \] is not convex, so \(f\) is not convex. No contradiction with the rule: \(\tilde h\) is not nondecreasing, since \(\tilde h(0)=+\infty>\tilde h(1.5)=0\).B&V §3.2.4 · p. 85, Remark 3.3
!

\(h\) nondecreasing on its domain is not enough. The moment the \(+\infty\) extension sits to the left of a finite value, \(\tilde h\) is no longer monotone — and the domain of \(f\) can shatter into disconnected pieces.

§7vector case (k ≥ 1)

When \(g:\mathbf R^n\to\mathbf R^k\) and \(h:\mathbf R^k\to\mathbf R\), the same story holds argument-by-argument. The twice-differentiable check reads:

\[ f''(x)=g'(x)^{\mathsf T}\,\nabla^2 h(g(x))\,g'(x)+\nabla h(g(x))^{\mathsf T}\,g''(x). \tag{3.15}\] The monotonicity condition becomes "\(\tilde h\) nondecreasing in each argument" — equivalently \(\operatorname{dom}h-\mathbf R^k_+=\operatorname{dom}h\) for convex \(h\). Then \(h\) convex, nondecreasing in each argument, and each \(g_i\) convex \(\Rightarrow f\) convex; and the mirror rules.B&V §3.2.4 · p. 86, eq. 3.15

Example 3.14. Three staples fall straight out: the sum of the \(r\) largest of convex \(g_i\) is convex; \(\log\sum_i e^{g_i}\) is convex when each \(g_i\) is convex; and the geometric mean \(\big(\prod_i g_i\big)^{1/k}\) is concave when each \(g_i\) is concave and nonnegative.

log-sum-exp of convex gᵢconvex

\(h(y)=\log\sum_i e^{y_i}\) is convex and nondecreasing in each \(y_i\); composing with convex \(g_i\) keeps convexity. This is the smooth-max you meet everywhere in logistic and softmax models.

§8expert lens — what to keep straight (diagnose)

D1The four rules

\(f\) inherits \(h\)'s curvature. For a convex \(f\): pair convex \(g\) ↔ nondecreasing \(\tilde h\), concave \(g\) ↔ nonincreasing \(\tilde h\). Backwards pairing is the classic error.

D2Monotonicity on h̃, not h

And the domain direction is curvature-dependent: convex \(h\) → extends left for nondecreasing; concave \(h\) → extends right (e.g. \(\log\)). See the §2 table.

D3Remark 3.3 is the test

\(g=x^2\) into \(h=0\) on \([1,2]\) gives a non-convex \(\operatorname{dom}f\). If you can explain why, you've internalized "\(\tilde h\), not \(h\)."

D4The affine special case

§3.2.2 is this rule with an affine inner \(g(x)=Ax+b\): the Jensen step holds with equality, so no outer monotonicity is needed.

D5Reach-for-it

\(\exp g\), \(\log g\), \(1/g\), \(g^p\) (\(p\ge1\)), \(-\log(-g)\). Vector: log-sum-exp of convex; geometric mean of concave nonnegative.

Main points for quick review

Rules

\(h\circ g\) convex if [\(h\) convex, \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing, \(g\) convex] or [\(h\) convex, \(\tilde h\) nonincreasing, \(g\) concave]; concave mirrors. \(f\) inherits \(h\)'s curvature; \(g\) matches \(h\)'s monotonicity.

Why (twice-diff)

\(f''=h''g'^2+h'g''\) (3.9), sign-read the two terms.

Crucial

Monotonicity on \(\tilde h\); domain-direction is curvature-dependent (convex \(h\) nondec → left; concave \(h\) nondec → right, e.g. \(\log\)). Remark 3.3: \(g=x^2\) into \(h=0\) on \([1,2]\) → non-convex \(\operatorname{dom}f\).

Proof

(3.12) \(g\) convex → (3.13) \(\tilde h\) nondecreasing → (3.14) \(h\) convex.

Ex 3.13

\(\exp g\), \(\log g\), \(1/g\), \(g^p\), \(-\log(-g)\). Vector: log-sum-exp; geometric mean.
