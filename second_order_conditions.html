Second-order
conditions

For twice-differentiable \(f\), convexity becomes a curvature test: the Hessian \(\nabla^2 f(x)\) must be positive semidefinite at every point — and \(\operatorname{dom}f\) must be convex. Two famous traps hide here: strictness is one-way, and the domain clause cannot be dropped.

∇²f(x) ⪰ 0 for all x quadratic: ∇²f = P upward curvature p. 70–71 · mastery: diagnose
§0where we are

§3.1.3 gave the first-order (gradient) test: convexity as "the tangent underestimates". §3.1.4 gives the second-order (Hessian) test: for twice-differentiable \(f\), convexity \(\iff \nabla^2 f \succeq 0\) everywhere. B&V leaves the proof as exercise 3.8, so the work here is in applying the test — above all to quadratics — plus the two famous traps: strictness is only one-way, and the domain clause is not decoration.

§1the second-order condition

Suppose \(f\) is twice differentiable: its Hessian \(\nabla^2 f\) exists at every point of \(\operatorname{dom}f\), which is open. Then:

\(f\) is convex if and only if \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is convex and its Hessian is positive semidefinite: \[ \nabla^2 f(x)\ \succeq\ 0\qquad\text{for all }x\in\operatorname{dom}f. \]B&V §3.1.4 · L3559–3563

Two clauses again — a convex domain and the matrix inequality, at every point, not just one.

!

\(\succeq 0\) is the Löwner order, not "entries \(\ge 0\)". It means \(\nabla^2 f(x)\) lies in the positive semidefinite cone \(\mathbf{S}^n_+\) (§2.2.5): \(z^{\mathsf T}\nabla^2 f(x)\,z\ge 0\) for all \(z\), equivalently all eigenvalues \(\ge 0\). A matrix with every entry positive can easily fail it — and \(\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}\succeq 0\) has zero entries.

§2scalar case & geometry
For \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) the condition reduces to the familiar one: \(f''(x)\ge 0\) for all \(x\), and \(\operatorname{dom}f\) an interval.B&V §3.1.4 · L3564–3568
  • calculus
    \(f''\ge 0\) says the derivative \(f'\) is nondecreasing — slopes only turn upward as you move right.
  • geometry
    \(\nabla^2 f(x)\succeq 0\) means the graph of \(f\) has positive (upward) curvature at \(x\) — it bends away from its tangent, never toward it.
  • domain
    In one variable "convex domain" just means an interval. Keep it: it is exactly what \(1/x^2\) violates (§5).

§3concave & strict variants
  • concave
    \(f\) is concave if and only if \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is convex and \[ \nabla^2 f(x)\ \preceq\ 0\quad\text{for all }x\in\operatorname{dom}f \] — negative semidefinite Hessian, i.e. downward curvature.
  • strict
    If \(\nabla^2 f(x)\ \succ\ 0\) (positive definite) for all \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\), then \(f\) is strictly convex. The implication runs one way only.
The converse of the strict condition is false: \(f(x)=x^4\) is strictly convex, but \(f''(0)=0\), so \(\nabla^2 f\succ 0\) fails at the origin. Positive definiteness is sufficient, not necessary, for strict convexity.B&V §3.1.4 · L3569–3576
!

The asymmetry to memorize. Convexity \(\iff \nabla^2 f\succeq 0\) is an equivalence. Strict convexity \(\Longleftarrow \nabla^2 f\succ 0\) is only an implication. A strictly convex function may have a Hessian that goes singular at isolated points (\(x^4\) at \(0\)); it may not go indefinite anywhere, since it is still convex.

§4example 3.2 — quadratic functions

Take the general quadratic on \(\mathbb{R}^n\), with \(P\in\mathbf{S}^n\), \(q\in\mathbb{R}^n\), \(r\in\mathbb{R}\):

\[ f(x)=\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Px+q^{\mathsf T}x+r,\qquad \nabla f(x)=Px+q,\qquad \nabla^2 f(x)=P. \] The Hessian is constant — it does not depend on \(x\). Hence \(f\) is convex \(\iff P\succeq 0\); concave \(\iff P\preceq 0\).B&V §3.1.4 · Example 3.2 · L3578–3585

And because the Hessian is constant, the strict case closes into a clean equivalence — the \(x^4\)-style gap cannot open up when \(\nabla^2 f\) is the same matrix everywhere:

  • strictly convex
    \(\iff P\succ 0\) (all eigenvalues \(>0\))
  • strictly concave
    \(\iff P\prec 0\) (all eigenvalues \(<0\))
i

So classifying a quadratic is entirely a question about \(P\): compute its eigenvalues (or run the leading-principal-minor test). \(q\) and \(r\) shift and lift the graph — they never touch curvature.

§5remark 3.1 — the domain clause cannot be dropped

It is tempting to read the theorem as "\(\nabla^2 f\succeq 0\) on \(\operatorname{dom}f\) ⟹ convex". It is not. B&V's remark makes the point with one function:

\[ f(x)=\frac{1}{x^2},\qquad \operatorname{dom}f=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\ne 0\},\qquad f''(x)=\frac{6}{x^4}>0\ \ \text{for all }x\in\operatorname{dom}f. \] Yet \(f\) is not convex — because \(\operatorname{dom}f\) is not convex.B&V §3.1.4 · Remark 3.1 · L3587–3591

The witness: take \(x=-1\) and \(y=1\), both in \(\operatorname{dom}f\). Their midpoint is \(0\), which is not in \(\operatorname{dom}f\) — the punctured line is not a convex set — so the defining inequality \(f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)\) cannot even be stated at \(\theta=1/2\), let alone hold. The curvature condition passes; the function still fails to be convex.

!

Second-order tests are local; convexity is global. The Hessian only sees each point in isolation. What glues the local information into a global statement is convexity of the domain — it guarantees that the segment between any two points stays inside \(\operatorname{dom}f\). Delete that clause and the theorem is simply false. (Note \(1/x^2\) is convex on each branch \(x>0\) and \(x<0\) separately — each is an interval.)

§6worked examples
Example 1 — classify three 2×2 quadraticseigenvalues of P

With \(f(x)=\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Px\), so \(\nabla^2 f=P\), and \(\lambda_{1,2}=\tfrac12\big(\operatorname{tr}P\pm\sqrt{(\operatorname{tr}P)^2-4\det P}\big)\):

\[ P_a=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}:\ \lambda=3,1>0\ \Rightarrow\ P_a\succ 0\ \Rightarrow\ f\ \text{strictly convex.} \]

\[ P_b=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}:\ \lambda=2,0\ \Rightarrow\ P_b\succeq 0\ \text{but not}\ \succ 0\ \Rightarrow\ f\ \text{convex, not strictly} \] (it is flat along \(z=(1,-1)\): \(z^{\mathsf T}P_b z=0\)).

\[ P_c=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}:\ \lambda=3,-1\ \Rightarrow\ \text{indefinite}\ \Rightarrow\ f\ \text{neither convex nor concave.} \]

Note \(P_c\) has every entry positive and still fails \(\succeq 0\): with \(z=(1,-1)\), \[ z^{\mathsf T}P_c z = 1 + 2(2)(1)(-1) + 1 = -2\ <\ 0. \] Read \(\succeq\) as an eigenvalue / quadratic-form statement, never entrywise.

Example 2 — x⁴: strictly convex, yet f″(0) = 0the converse fails

\(f(x)=x^4\) on \(\mathbb{R}\): \(f'(x)=4x^3\), \(f''(x)=12x^2\ge 0\), so \(f\) is convex by the second-order condition. It is in fact strictly convex (its chords lie strictly above, and \(f'\) is strictly increasing). But

\[ f''(0)=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nabla^2 f\succ 0\ \text{fails at }x=0. \]

So "strictly convex ⟹ \(\nabla^2 f\succ 0\)" is false. Only the forward direction holds. Contrast the quadratic \(\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Px\), where the Hessian is constant and strict convexity is equivalent to \(P\succ 0\) — the gap needs a Hessian that varies with \(x\) to open at all.

Example 3 — the 1/x² domain remark, spelled outx = −1, y = 1

\(f(x)=1/x^2\), \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x\ne 0\}\). Compute: \(f'(x)=-2x^{-3}\), \(f''(x)=6x^{-4}=6/x^4>0\) on the whole domain. Second-order condition on the Hessian: passes.

Take \(x=-1,\ y=1,\ \theta=\tfrac12\). Then \(f(x)=f(y)=1\), so the right-hand side of the convexity inequality is \(1\). But the left-hand side is \(f(0)\), and \(0\notin\operatorname{dom}f\):

\[ \theta x+(1-\theta)y=0\notin\operatorname{dom}f\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \operatorname{dom}f\ \text{is not convex}\ \Longrightarrow\ f\ \text{is not convex.} \]

Restrict to \(\operatorname{dom}f=\{x>0\}\) (an interval) and the very same function is convex. The domain clause carries real weight.

§7expert lens — diagnose

D1The test

\(f\) twice differentiable: convex \(\iff \nabla^2 f(x)\succeq 0\) for all \(x\) and \(\operatorname{dom}f\) convex. Scalar: \(f''\ge 0\). Concave: \(\nabla^2 f\preceq 0\).

D2Strict is one-way

\(\nabla^2 f\succ 0\) everywhere \(\Longrightarrow\) strictly convex. Not conversely: \(x^4\) is strictly convex with \(f''(0)=0\).

D3Quadratics

\(f=\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Px+q^{\mathsf T}x+r\) has \(\nabla^2 f=P\) (constant): convex \(\iff P\succeq 0\), strictly convex \(\iff P\succ 0\). Here strictness is an equivalence.

D4The domain clause is real

\(1/x^2\) on \(\{x\ne 0\}\) has \(f''>0\) everywhere and is still not convex — the punctured line is not a convex set.

D5\(\succeq 0\) is not entrywise

It is the PSD-cone / eigenvalue condition \(z^{\mathsf T}\nabla^2 f\,z\ge 0\ \forall z\), not "all entries \(\ge 0\)". \(\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}\) is all-positive and indefinite.

Main points for quick review

The condition

\(f\) twice diff.: convex \(\iff \operatorname{dom}f\) convex and \(\nabla^2 f(x)\succeq 0\) for all \(x\). Scalar: \(f''\ge 0\). Concave: \(\preceq 0\).

Strictness

\(\nabla^2 f\succ 0\) everywhere \(\Rightarrow\) strictly convex — but not conversely (\(x^4\): strictly convex, \(f''(0)=0\)).

Quadratics

\(\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Px+q^{\mathsf T}x+r\): \(\nabla^2 f=P\); convex \(\iff P\succeq 0\), strictly convex \(\iff P\succ 0\).

Domain

Essential: \(1/x^2\) has \(f''>0\) on \(\{x\ne0\}\) but isn't convex — nonconvex domain.

Reading ⪰

The PSD-cone (Löwner) order — eigenvalues \(\ge 0\) — not entrywise \(\ge 0\).
