Jensen's inequality and extensions

The defining inequality (3.1), climbed all the way up: two points → k points → integrals → expectations. The general form f(E x) ≤ E f(x) doesn't merely follow from convexity — it characterizes it.

f(E x) — function of the mean E f(x) — mean of the function convex ⟺ f(E x) ≤ E f(x) p. 77–78 · mastery: state (3.5)
§0where we are

§3.1.7 gave the exact set-theoretic bridge: f convex ⟺ epi f convex. §3.1.8 climbs a different ladder — not to sets but to averages. The basic two-point inequality (3.1) is, under another name, Jensen's inequality; it extends to k-point convex combinations, to infinite sums, to integrals, and finally to expectations. The top of the ladder, f(E x) ≤ E f(x), turns out to contain the bottom rung and to characterize convexity outright.

§1(3.1) is Jensen — and the k-point extension

The defining inequality of a convex function is, under another name, Jensen's inequality:

\[ f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)\le\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y). \]B&V §3.1.8 · (3.1) · L3918–3925

It extends without effort to convex combinations of more than two points:

If \(f\) is convex, \(x_1,\dots,x_k\in\operatorname{dom}f\), and \(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_k\ge0\) with \(\theta_1+\cdots+\theta_k=1\), then \[ f(\theta_1x_1+\cdots+\theta_kx_k)\le\theta_1f(x_1)+\cdots+\theta_kf(x_k). \]B&V §3.1.8 · L3918–3925
!

Read the hypotheses exactly. The weights must be nonnegative and must sum to 1. Drop either condition and the inequality is false — these are not bookkeeping, they are the statement. A weight that sneaks below zero flips the corresponding term's contribution; weights summing to more or less than 1 no longer place \(\sum\theta_ix_i\) inside the convex hull at all.

§2integrals and expectations — the general form

Exactly as convex-set combinations climbed from two points to infinite sums, integrals, and mixtures, the inequality climbs too — provided the objects on both sides exist.

  • integral
    If \(p(x)\ge0\) on \(S\subseteq\operatorname{dom}f\) with \(\int_S p(x)\,dx=1\), then \[ f\!\left(\int_S p(x)\,x\,dx\right)\le\int_S f(x)\,p(x)\,dx, \] provided the integrals exist. The density plays the role of the weights: nonnegative, total mass 1.
  • expectation
    If \(x\) is a random variable with \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\) with probability one, and \(f\) is convex, then \[ f(\mathbf{E}\,x)\le\mathbf{E}\,f(x), \tag{3.5} \] provided the expectations exist.
\[ f(\mathbf{E}\,x)\;\le\;\mathbf{E}\,f(x)\qquad\text{(3.5)} \]B&V §3.1.8 · (3.5) · L3926–3936
!

The provisos are part of the statement, not fine print. For (3.5) you need \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\) almost surely (probability one, not merely “usually”), and both expectations must exist. For the integral form you need \(p\ge0\), \(\int_S p=1\), \(S\subseteq\operatorname{dom}f\), and existence of both integrals. Quoting (3.5) without them is quoting a different, false, theorem.

Notice the shape of every rung on the ladder: a nonnegative weighting of total mass one, pushed through \(f\). Two-point weights \((\theta,1-\theta)\), \(k\)-point weights \(\theta_i\), a density \(p\), a probability measure — the same object at four levels of generality.

§3(3.5) characterizes convexity — the headline

The general form is not a weaker consequence of the basic one — it contains it. Let x be the two-point random variable with prob(x = x₁) = θ and prob(x = x₂) = 1 − θ:

Recovering (3.1) from (3.5)the general contains the basic
\[ \mathbf{E}\,x=\theta x_1+(1-\theta)x_2 \]
mean of the two-point variable is exactly the convex combination
\[ \mathbf{E}\,f(x)=\theta f(x_1)+(1-\theta)f(x_2) \]
the same two atoms, evaluated through \(f\)
\[ f\big(\theta x_1+(1-\theta)x_2\big)\le\theta f(x_1)+(1-\theta)f(x_2) \]
so (3.5) collapses to (3.1) — the two-point case. ∎

And now the converse, which is what makes this section more than a generalization exercise:

If \(f\) is not convex, there is a random variable \(x\), with \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\) with probability one, such that \(f(\mathbf{E}\,x)>\mathbf{E}\,f(x)\).B&V §3.1.8 · L3937–3947
!

Therefore (3.5) is a characterization. \(f\) is convex if and only if \(f(\mathbf{E}\,x)\le\mathbf{E}\,f(x)\) for every random variable \(x\) lying in \(\operatorname{dom}f\) with probability one (and with the expectations existing). Convexity is not just sufficient for Jensen — it is equivalent to it.

Historical noteJensen: the midpoint case only

All of the inequalities above — the two-point (3.1), the \(k\)-point extension, the integral form, and (3.5) — now go by the name Jensen's inequality. Jensen himself studied only the midpoint case \(f\!\left(\tfrac{x+y}{2}\right)\le\tfrac{f(x)+f(y)}{2}\). The name has since been attached to the whole family.

§4Remark 3.2 — dithering can't help
Let \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\) and let \(z\) be any zero-mean random vector (with \(x+z\in\operatorname{dom}f\) with probability one and \(\mathbf{E}f(x+z)\) existing). Then \[ \mathbf{E}\,f(x+z)\ \ge\ f(x). \]B&V §3.1.8 · Remark 3.2 · L3948–3957
Proof — one line from (3.5)apply (3.5) to x + z
\[ \mathbf{E}\,f(x+z)\ \ge\ f\big(\mathbf{E}(x+z)\big) \]
(3.5) applied to the random variable \(x+z\)
\[ f\big(\mathbf{E}(x+z)\big)=f(x+\mathbf{E}z)=f(x+0)=f(x) \]
linearity of expectation and \(\mathbf{E}z=0\). ∎

Interpretation. Randomization or dithering — adding zero-mean noise to the argument — cannot decrease a convex function on average. It can only hurt, or at best break even: for affine \(f\) the inequality is an equality, since \(\mathbf{E}f(x+z)=f(x)+\nabla f^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{E}z=f(x)\). There is never an average gain to be had from jittering the input of a convex objective.

§5expert lens — what to keep straight (diagnose)

D1Direction

f(E x) ≤ E f(x) for convex f — function of the mean ≤ mean of the function. Reversed for concave. Getting this backwards is the classic error.

D2Hypotheses are the statement

k-point needs θᵢ ≥ 0 and Σθᵢ = 1; (3.5) needs x ∈ dom f w.p. 1 and the expectations to exist. Integral form: p ≥ 0, ∫p = 1, integrals exist.

D3(3.5) is a CHARACTERIZATION

Not a consequence. Strength ladder: §3.1.6 sublevel convexity is only necessary; §3.1.7 epigraph convexity is a biconditional; (3.5) is a characterization too — convex ⟺ Jensen for every admissible x.

D4(3.1) is the two-point case

Recover it with prob(x = x₁) = θ, prob(x = x₂) = 1 − θ: then E x and E f(x) are literally the two sides of (3.1).

D5Remark 3.2

E f(x + z) ≥ f(x) for zero-mean z — one line from (3.5) plus E z = 0. Dithering can't lower a convex function on average; affine breaks even.

Main points for quick review

The ladder

Jensen = the defining inequality (3.1); extends to \(k\)-point convex combinations (\(\theta_i\ge0\), \(\sum\theta_i=1\)), to integrals, and to expectations.

General form (3.5)

\(f(\mathbf{E}x)\le\mathbf{E}f(x)\) for convex \(f\), with \(x\in\operatorname{dom}f\) w.p. 1 and the expectations existing.

It characterizes convexity

An iff: if \(f\) isn't convex there exists \(x\) with \(f(\mathbf{E}x)>\mathbf{E}f(x)\). And (3.1) is the two-point case of (3.5).

Remark 3.2

\(\mathbf{E}f(x+z)\ge f(x)\) for zero-mean \(z\) — dithering can't lower a convex function on average (affine: equality).
